Introduction¶

One of my favorite kind of movies is, as you might have guessed it already, horror movies. I’ve been a fan since my childhood and the only thing that has changes is that now I can sleep alone after seeing one.

I must say that despite being a fan, I have to agree with the idea that the horror movies are not the best ones out there. But I am also aware that since, as a fan, I’ve watched many, it’s quite natural that I’ve seen a considerable chunk of bad ones.

So, how bad are the horror movies compared to the rest?

In the analysis of the IMDB data I will try to find some answers to these questions:

  1. How are horror movies compared to the other genres in terms of rating and financial numbers?
  2. Are any horror sub-genres better that others?
  3. Are big names in the industry (actors and directors) sharing their talents with the horror genre?

Data Cleaning: 1. drop columns we do not need¶

'imdb_id' - there are missing values and we do not need to join with some other IMDB database; we will use the id, which has no missing values if we need to single out a specific line.
'popularity' - this represents the frequency of accessing that movie in the current week - so we would actually need a list of popularity /week if we were to extract some knowledge. The popularity of the who-knows-which week of the data extraction is not really relevant.
'budget' and 'revenue' - we also have the same measures adjusted for inflation which will help us compare apples with apples, so we don't need the original measures.
'homepage'- we won't be surfing the internet, so don't need for them.
'tagline'- besides missing values, we already have keywords and overview, so it should be enough to extract some pertinent "tagging" of the movies. Usually taglines tend to be marketing catchphrases, not really informative statements.
'production_companies' - I won't be using these because nowadays there's HBO, Netflix and Amazon :).
'release_date' - we already have the release_year and since we have a considerable time-span (1960-2015) we won't need date level of granularity.

Data Cleaning: 3. Dropping rows - Missing data, Duplicates, Irrelevant data¶

I'll start with the latter -Irrelevant data: The central limit theorem rule of thumb sais if the data is normally distributed, you need a sample of size > 30 so your results (average for instance) are relevant for the population.
Many of the mean average ratings in the dataset are the result of less than 30 votes - if you look at the 25th percentile you will see it's 17 votes and the 50th is 38. Moreover, we do not know if the rating scores in the population are normally distributed (if not, than 30 counts would be insufficient).
Based on the IMDB statements, they are actually using a weighted average algorithm that is secret so we should probably trust the relevance of the rating even for the ones with less than 30 votes. However... let's compare the average of the movies with less than 30 votes vs the overall sample, and see if the difference is significant.

Conclusions¶

First let me point out some caveats:

  • Since I decided to remove all records of the dataset with 0 revenue and budget, it is possible that the results I got are far from the real world view. Take into account that the purpose was to analyze the current dataset so no inference should be made based on it.
  • The analysis of star directors and actors preference for some genres is mainly an exercise into data manipulation and at most a very accurate portrayal of my personal views on what makes a star.
  • Although I noticed some patterns in the data, the analysis does not suggest that the findings have any relevance in the real world.
  • The data distributions look pretty neat so there might be some non-linear correlation that I still need to learn about.

What I have found:

  • Horror movies have an average rating only a little lower than the other movies (8% lower), but also they have less top rates.
  • Horror movies receive, on average, half the budget vs what other movies receive and they also bring half the revenues.
  • There's no linear correlation between the amount invested in a movie and the revenue it brings, no matter the genre.
  • The higher rating for the horror movies is not linearly correlated with higher earnings, but for the other genres there is a hint that better rating will bring some money. On the other hand, if the rating is low, there's very little chance that movie will get good revenues.
  • The biggest spenders and earners are actually the average rated movies.
  • The most common themes in horror movies are devil/demons/ghosts and psycho killers - and the psychos actually bring the best profits.
  • The stars (as defined by me) are found more often in other genres than in horrors.

In summary, I would say horror movies are actually not as bad as I imagined; a few actually have made remarkable revenues and received very good ratings. In the future I would like to see more "stars" in the credit lines of horror movies.
